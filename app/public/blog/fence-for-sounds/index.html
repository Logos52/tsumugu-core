A Fence for Sounds

2026-07-02 · 3 min read

Sound components are supposed to be one of the script's great gifts: the part of a character that tells you roughly how it sounds. Then a learner meets 批 (pī) and finds 比 (bǐ) inside it, or meets 悲 (bēi) built on 非 (fēi), and the gift looks broken. The pinyin letters disagree, the connection gets discarded, and the whole phonetic system quietly loses credibility, one seemingly failed match at a time.

The matches are not failed; they are old. Sound components record what words sounded like when the characters were coined, and centuries of sound change have pulled the modern readings apart. The drift is not random. Family-wide, it collapses into a small set of related initials that substitute for one another, seven groups in all: {t, d}, {zh, ch, sh}, {j, q, x}, {b, p, f}, {g, k, h}, {z, c, s}, and {m, w}. The 非 family shows one group at work: 菲 fēi, 排 pái, 悲 bēi, 輩 bèi, with f, p, and b trading places while the family stays one family. The same swap runs through European languages, father against Vater against vader; Mandarin is one instance of ordinary drift.

The strange part is what installing the groups feels like. The list stops no sound change and adds no new data, yet once it stands as an authoritative statement that these initials belong together, links that used to read as broken start absorbing on their own. There is a well-worn story about a daycare whose children ignored a playground near a busy road until a fence went up; the fence stopped no cars, and the children played to the edge. A declared boundary is what makes territory usable. The seven groups are that fence for phonology: with the boundary declared, 批 under 比 reads as family, and a trained learner starts perceiving links that untrained native speakers cannot see.

The fence stops no sound change; it makes the territory usable.

Doubt gets resolved with the mouth, not the eye. When two initials seem unrelated, pronouncing both and noticing where the tongue or lips make contact settles it: t and d strike the same ridge, b and p share the lips. The groups are physical neighborhoods, which is why they held together through millennia of drift.

Two honest limits keep the tool trusted. Tone stays outside the fence: the pairings were made in a language that likely had no tones, so a series is tone-blind, and every tone gets learned as its own fact. And series are not uniform; members can scatter across a group's edges, vowels wobble, and the occasional irregular member turns up. Flagging an exception preserves trust in the rest of the system better than pretending the system is exceptionless.

The characters keep coming with their old sounds sealed inside them, and the drift is not going to un-drift. What the seven groups change is what a mismatched initial means. Before the fence, it read as another broken promise from the phonetic system. Behind the fence, it reads as a family member wearing the group's other initial, and each new instance of the pattern soaks in almost effortlessly.
