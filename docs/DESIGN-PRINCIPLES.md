# Tsumugu — Design Principles

A short, binding set for the product's visual and interaction design. When a
choice is in question, it should satisfy these. Distilled 2026-07-03 during the
library redesign (Apple-minimal system, spectral teal-blue accent, book covers +
catalog). The copy principles align with the vault's Writing Standards.

Current visual system: cool neutral palette, one spectral teal-blue accent,
Inter / PingFang TC for the interface, LXGW WenKai TC reserved for reading text,
rounded surfaces with soft depth. This supersedes the earlier antique "Bound
Volume" serif direction for the app's product surfaces.

---

## 1. Show, don't tell
The interface demonstrates; copy adds only what the screen cannot show. Never
label what is already visible. Cut taglines and captions that narrate the layout
— "Read by textbook, by level, or explore everything" when those three surfaces
are right there on the page.
**Test:** delete the sentence. If nothing is lost, it was telling.

## 2. No redundancy
Say a thing once. No word repeated down a list (a status column reading
"read · read · read"), no section restated in its own subtitle, no fact placed
in two spots. Redundancy is the most frequent defect here — hunt it every pass.

## 3. Whitespace has a job
Empty space is design material. Every gap is either deliberate breathing room or
a place that owes the reader something — the space between a lesson title and its
status is a table-of-contents dot leader, not a void. A gap that reads as "I
didn't know what to put here" is a bug: give it meaning or close it.

## 4. Timeless over trendy
Reach for durable, real conventions — a shelf of books, a table of contents, dot
leaders, an obi fact-line — never a clever signature motif or an of-the-moment
effect. **Test:** will this look considered in ten years? Minimal precision ages
well; novelty rots.

## 5. One accent, held quiet
A single accent colour is the only saturated voice; everything else is faint grey
and ink. The accent marks state and the one action. It never decorates and never
glows — tone it down in dark mode, where it reads brighter. If two things are
accented, one of them should not be.

*Signifier nuance (Basecamp / Ulysses):* a small set of **faint, functional**
signifier colours and icons is allowed for the categories a reader navigates by
— reading format, level — carried only on small glyphs, dots, or tags, never on
fills or large areas. Kept faint, they read as quiet identity and wayfinding, not
decoration; the teal accent stays reserved for action and state.

## 6. Follow the cultural grain
Chinese reads top-down: a mark goes on top, never on the left spine. Books look
like books. LXGW WenKai carries what you *read*; the sans carries the *interface*.
Taiwanese, not mainland-coded — no red.

## 7. Explore, don't funnel
The library is a place to wander. There is a main quest (a textbook, cover to
cover) and side quests (loose readings by level or topic), and not everyone is on
the same one. Offer the ways in as equals — by book, by level, by browsing.
"Continue" is a convenience, never the spine of the page.

## 8. Progressive depth
The library opens up as the reader travels. A newcomer meets a calm, shallow
surface; more depth appears as they progress. Never lay the whole map in front of
everyone at once — depth earns its way in.

## 9. Design the cold start first
Build the empty, fresh, nothing-logged state first — a brand-new visitor with no
history, no progress, no "continue." That is the state most people meet first and
the one most often left ugly. The progress-rich view is an enhancement layered on
top, never the baseline.

## 10. Minimal is precise
Elegance is a restrained vision executed exactly — spacing, type scale, alignment,
detail. Every element that remains is deliberate. Match the execution to the
vision; a minimal direction demands more precision, not more parts.

## 11. The interface never speaks about itself
The chrome carries no self-narration. Not the product's production method
("AI-original", "original corpus"), not its materials (type credits), not its
terms ("free · $0 · no account"), not its mechanics ("tap a word for its
meaning"), not its architecture ("two scaffolding rails"), not a legend
decoding its own marks. A well-made object doesn't annotate itself: the marks
teach by being used, the price is answered where buying happens, the making
belongs in the colophon — the **About page is the one room where the product
may talk about itself**, because that is what the reader came there for.
**Test:** if a line's subject is the product rather than the reader's text or
task, cut it. This is Show-don't-tell (§1) at the level of voice: §1 forbids
narrating the layout; §11 forbids narrating the product.
