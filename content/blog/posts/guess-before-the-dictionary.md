Meeting an unfamiliar character usually goes one way: straight to the dictionary. The lookup takes three seconds, the reading and meaning appear, a card maybe gets made, and the reading fades within days. It is one of the quiet frustrations of the intermediate years, the same characters looked up over and over, arriving and leaving without a trace.

The trace is missing because nothing was processed. Committing to a guess before the answer arrives is what forces the mind to actually work the material: connections get hunted, candidates weighed, and the eventual answer lands on prepared ground, confirming or correcting a position instead of filling a blank. Establishing the problem before the answer moves practice transfer from roughly one-to-one toward ten-to-one. The guess is not a detour on the way to the lookup. The guess is the encoding.

For characters, the guess has a proper structure, because most characters carry two predictions in their parts. Meeting 棠 cold: the 木 at the bottom points at a tree or wood domain, and the 尚 on top narrows the syllable to the family it writes elsewhere, one of shang, chang, zhang, tang, or dang. Five syllables and a meaning domain is a real position, committed before the dictionary opens. The lookup settles it: táng, the birchleaf pear, a tree. The domain held, the syllable was in the list, and the tone, which structure rarely predicts, gets learned as a separate fact. A character met this way arrives wired into the component system that predicted it, which is why it stays.

```text
棠, met cold
meaning side: 木 below → tree or wood domain
sound side:   尚 above → {shang, chang, zhang, tang, dang}
commit → look up → táng, "birchleaf pear," a tree; tone learned as its own fact
```

The commitment is the part that does the work, and it is also the part that gets skipped. Reading the components and vaguely feeling that several syllables are possible is not a guess; it is a lookup with extra steps. The move is to state the position, aloud or on paper, before checking: tree domain, probably tang or dang. Wrong guesses are not failures of the method. A wrong guess corrected is stronger encoding than a right answer received, because the correction lands exactly where the model was off.

The method has a floor and a price. It runs on component knowledge, so with few components known, most characters offer nothing to guess from, and building that base comes first. And it is slower per character than the bare lookup, a minute against three seconds, which stings exactly when reading momentum matters. Reserving the full guess for characters worth keeping, and letting throwaway lookups stay throwaway, is the honest balance.

**The arithmetic: a three-second lookup that fades, or a one-minute guess that moves transfer toward 10:1.**

Unfamiliar characters keep coming regardless; the dictionary will always be three seconds away. What the guess changes is what a lookup leaves behind. Received answers evaporate, and corrected positions accumulate, so the same three-second lookup either closes an open question or opens and closes nothing.
