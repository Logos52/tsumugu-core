Every review session with Chinese characters produces the same verdicts: this one I know, that one I forgot. The card gets its grade, the scheduler does its arithmetic, and the forgotten character comes back sooner. It feels rigorous. But "I forgot 頓" is a strangely blunt sentence, on a par with a mechanic saying the car is broken, and the bluntness has a cost that compounds across thousands of characters.

A character is not stored as one fact. It is one item with three separately stored links: the form to the sound, the form to the meaning, and the sound to the meaning. Each link strengthens on its own schedule and fades on its own schedule, and a learner can hold any two while the third is dark. That is why a single "do I know this character?" judgment hides more than it reveals. Seeing 頓 and recalling "pause" while the reading refuses to surface is not the same failure as hearing dùn and knowing the word while the written form will not come, and neither is the same as a form that reads fluently but connects to nothing.

**Store one item, train three links, and let the failure name the broken one.**

The blunt verdict matters because it buys the wrong repair. A failed card goes back into rotation whole, all three links re-drilled, though two of them were never broken. Across a large deck that is a steady tax of redundant reps, and worse, the broken link often is not even the one the card exercises. A card that always shows the form and asks for the meaning trains one direction of one link, and the sound-to-form link can rot underneath it for months, invisible, until handwriting exposes it.

The fix is to let the failure name its link before scheduling the repair. A form that looks familiar but will not sound out: the form-to-sound link, and the drill is reading aloud. A word known by ear whose character will not come: the sound-to-form link, and the drill is production, writing or recalling the form from the spoken word. A form whose meaning has gone vague: form-to-meaning, and the fix may be usage, not the card at all, since a meaning that only exists as a gloss was never firmly held. Testing the links separately in review, some cards asking form-to-sound, others sound-to-form, is what keeps a strong link from masking a dead one.

| The failure | The broken link | The drill |
| --- | --- | --- |
| The form looks familiar but will not sound out | Form → sound | Reading aloud |
| The word is known by ear but the form will not come | Sound → form | Producing the form from the spoken word |
| The form reads fluently but the meaning has gone vague | Form → meaning | Usage in real sentences, not the card |

The accounting has a price: more card types, a bit more bookkeeping, and reviews that take marginally longer because the diagnosis rides along. On a small deck the overhead is not worth it, and a beginner served by simple cards should keep them simple. The link view starts paying around the point where review volume is large enough that redundant reps cost real time.

Characters will keep being forgotten; three links per item across thousands of items guarantees it. What the link view changes is the size of a forgetting. Instead of a whole character lost and a whole character re-learned, it is usually one link down while two still hold, and a repair aimed at the one that broke.
