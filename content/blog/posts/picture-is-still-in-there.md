Beginners are usually told, somewhere in the first week, that Chinese characters started as pictures. Then they look at the characters. 日 (rì, "sun") is a box with a line in it. 耳 (ěr, "ear") could be a ladder. For most of the script, the promised pictures are nowhere to be seen, and the natural conclusion is that the picture story is a myth for children, leaving brute memorization of abstract stroke patterns as the honest path.

The pictures were real; the resemblance is what died. Millennia of stylization and copying wore the drawings down into strokes that print cleanly and depict nothing. But "depicts" is a claim about origin, not about the modern shape, and the origin is recoverable: the ancient forms sit in references one lookup away, and one look converts an arbitrary stroke pattern back into a scene.

**The pictures were real; the resemblance is what died, and one lookup restores the scene.**

取 (qǔ, "to take") can show what the conversion buys. In the modern glyph it reads as 耳 next to 又, two shapes with no apparent business together. The ancient form is a scene: 又 is a hand, and the character shows a hand seizing an ear, because ancient armies counted their kills in ears cut from the dead, easier to carry than heads. The scene is grisly and it does exactly what memory wants: a picturable event with the meaning inside it. From "take an ear" the senses extend to "take, get, receive, select." The character stops being six strokes in an arbitrary arrangement and becomes a picture drawn badly, which is a much lighter thing to remember.

```text
取 today:        耳 + 又 — two shapes with no apparent business together
ancient form:    a hand (又) seizing an ear (耳); armies counted kills in ears
sense chain:     take an ear → take, get → receive → select
```

The habit that follows costs one lookup per character, once, at first encounter. Meeting a new character means spending a few seconds with its ancient form, seeing the scene, and moving on. The lookup also protects against a quieter failure. When the real scene is absent, something fills the gap anyway, usually a folk parse of the modern shape, and folk parses are exactly the stories that collapse across a character's siblings. Consulting the record once beats inventing.

Two boundaries keep the habit honest. Some scenes are partly gone even at the root: in 美 (měi, "beautiful"), a person in a feather headdress, the headdress has decayed into a shape that resembles 羊 ("sheep"), and reading it as a sheep misreads the scene. The residue of corruption depicts nothing, and the ancient form is what says which parts still speak. And the habit is for characters whose form components carry the load; where a part is working purely as a sound cue, the picture hunt has nothing to find.

The script itself will keep looking abstract; no habit changes what the printed page shows. What changes is what sits behind it. A learner who has met the scenes reads 取 and briefly owns a hand and an ear, and the strokes on the page go back to being what they always were, the worn-down drawing of something.
