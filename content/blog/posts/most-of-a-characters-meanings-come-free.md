title: "Most of a Character's Meanings Come Free"

description: "Around 85% of dictionary senses derive from one original meaning; learn the root and the chain, and most of the list comes free."
---

Anyone who has looked up a common Chinese character in a serious dictionary has met the wall of glosses: eight, ten, twelve senses under one entry, numbered like a legal document. The natural reading of that list is a to-do list, twelve meanings to memorize for one character, multiplied by thousands of characters. It is one of the sights that makes literacy look unreachable from the beginning of the road.

The list is not what it looks like. Across a comprehensive dictionary, roughly 85% of senses derive from an older sense by ordinary metaphor and metonymy, the same moves English makes when "he hit the bottle" lets a container stand for what is in it. Another 18% or so arrived as sound loans, a form borrowed to write an unrelated word that merely sounded alike. Few senses are freestanding facts. Almost every entry is a family tree: one original meaning at the root, the one the form was actually drawn to depict, with everything else hanging off it in chains.

**Almost every gloss list is a family tree: one root, short chains, a loan or two circled.**

A famous character can show the whole shape. 漢 (hàn) today means "Chinese," and nothing about "Chinese" explains the water component 氵 sitting on its left. The root does: 漢 first named a river in central China. From the river came the people who lived beside it, from the people a dynasty, from the dynasty an ethnic group, and from the group an adult male of that group. Five senses, one memorization. The river explains the water, and each later sense is reachable from the one before it, the way "hall" reaches "the people in the hall."

```text
漢  a river in central China (hence 氵)
 →  the people living beside it
 →  the dynasty
 →  the ethnic group
 →  an adult male of that group
```

The practical rule that falls out is pruning: never memorize a sense that can be derived from a more basic one. For 長 in its zhǎng reading, storing "to grow" is enough, because context regenerates "grow up," "develop," "increase," and even "leader," a person grown in skill and usually older. Rare branches can wait until real text forces the issue. The load-bearing memorizations per character usually come down to the root sense plus a loan or two, and loans get marked as loans, a circled sense that connects by sound only, so the mind stops trying to invent a bridge that never existed.

The method has its costs and its edges. Finding the original meaning takes a source, since the root is a historical fact and not guessable from the modern form, and a small number of characters, under 2%, have roots that scholarship cannot recover, where the earliest attested sense stands in. The tree also does not learn itself: tracing a chain takes a few minutes per character, which is only worth spending on characters met often enough to repay it.

The dictionaries do not change; the wall of glosses will be there for the next character too. What changes is the price read off the wall. Twelve senses stop being twelve memorizations and start being one root, a chain of short steps, and a circled loan, and most of the list turns out to come free.
