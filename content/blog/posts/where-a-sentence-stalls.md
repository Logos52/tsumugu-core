---

For anyone speaking a second language, stalling mid-sentence is a regular event, and the frustrating version is the one where the words were all known. The meeting word, the tomorrow word, the change word: every piece sits in memory, reviewed and green, and the sentence still refuses to assemble before the moment passes. The usual conclusion is "my speaking is bad," which is true but not useful. Useful is knowing which layer the stall lives in, because each layer has a different repair, and the repairs do not substitute for each other.

A stall sits at one of three layers. The first is comprehension: the words themselves are missing or half-known, senses fuzzy, usage unknown. The second is approach: the words are known but the system for relating them is not there, no working model of how the language orders and connects its pieces. The third is execution: words known, model known, and retrieval still will not run at conversation speed. The layers stack, so a failure at one masquerades as a failure at the next one up, and drilling the wrong layer costs weeks.

**A stall has an address: comprehension, approach, or execution.**

A worked case makes the sort visible. A learner knows 會議 (huìyì, "meeting"), 明天 (míngtiān, "tomorrow"), and 改 (gǎi, "to change"), and wants to say the meeting moved to tomorrow. If the stall is not knowing those words, that is comprehension, and a card fixes it. If the words are there but no sentence shape suggests itself at all, that is approach: the missing piece is the pattern, not the vocabulary, and the fix is meeting the pattern in real sentences until it settles. But if a plausible sentence forms in the head two seconds after the moment needed it, the gap is execution. Everything was stored; retrieval ran too slowly. That gap is a skill gap, and it closes only through production under time pressure, speaking with a clock running, not through another card.

| The stall | The layer | The repair |
| --- | --- | --- |
| The words themselves are missing or fuzzy | Comprehension | Study: senses, usage, cards |
| Words known, but no sentence shape suggests itself | Approach | Meet the pattern in real sentences |
| The right sentence forms, two seconds too late | Execution | Production under time pressure |

The sort matters most because the third layer is the one that study habits systematically miss. Knowledge work feels productive and drilling under pressure feels uncomfortable, so execution gaps collect quietly while the deck grows. A learner who knows what to say and cannot say it in time does not need to know more.

The sort has a cost and a limit. It takes an honest minute after a failed exchange, when the temptation is to move on, and the layers blur at the edges: a pattern known slightly is both an approach gap and an execution gap, and the sort can only pick the dominant one. When the same stall keeps returning after its layer was drilled, the diagnosis was probably one layer too high.

Sentences will keep stalling; that part does not change. What the three layers change is what a stall leaves behind. Instead of a general verdict about bad speaking, each one can leave an address, and the next practice session can go to where the failure actually lives.
