Somewhere in most language journeys there is a stretch where the flashcards are going well and the speaking is not. The deck says hundreds of words are known, the reviews come back green, and then a real conversation starts and the sentences arrive late, or half-built, or not at all. It reads like a speaking problem, so the usual response is more speaking practice.

Sometimes that is the right call. But fluency is built out of two different stores, and they fail differently. One store is the inventory: which words exist, which senses each one carries, and when a native speaker would actually reach for it. That store is knowledge, the kind that can be explained, and in learning science it goes by declarative. The other store is the delivery: retrieving and assembling words in real time, fast enough that nobody waits. That one is a skill, the kind that runs without conscious thought, and it goes by procedural. Speech needs both at once. A missing word cannot be delivered at any speed, and a word that is stored but not automated arrives too slowly to use.

**Fluency runs on two stores, an inventory and a delivery, and they fail differently.**

The two gate each other, which is what makes the diagnosis worth a minute. Delivery drills can only automate what the inventory already holds, so drilling output against a thin inventory polishes a small vocabulary to a high shine and stalls there. And inventory work alone stocks a warehouse with no loading dock: everything is in there, and nothing comes out on time.

The cleanest way to feel the difference is to put two failures side by side, ones that look identical from the outside. Wanting to say "we had a meeting" and not knowing that Chinese reaches for 開會 (kāihuì), literally "open a meeting," is an inventory gap. No quantity of speaking reps produces a word that was never stored, and the fix is learning which verb the language actually pairs with meetings. Knowing 開會 cold on a flashcard and still stalling mid-sentence is the delivery gap, and there the fix is reps: producing the word inside sentences, under time pressure, until it stops being a decision.

Sorting one from the other takes less machinery than it might seem to need. The whole diagnostic is one question: can the missing thing be explained? A gap that can be stated ("I did not know 開 was the verb") is inventory, and study fixes it. A gap that can only be felt ("I knew it and it would not come out") is delivery, and production fixes it. Explanation, however good, only ever fills the first store. The second is built by doing, and by nothing else.

| The gap | The store | The fix |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Explainable: "I did not know 開 was the verb" | Inventory | Study: senses, usage, cards |
| Felt only: "I knew it and it would not come out" | Delivery | Production under time pressure |

The model does charge a fee, and the fee is mostly patience: a small delay before every fix, spent classifying the failure instead of grabbing the nearest drill. It can also be over-run. Plenty of output problems are exactly what they look like, and a learner who audits the inventory after every stumble is stalling in a different way.

The stalls in conversation keep coming either way, and no amount of diagnosis prevents them. What the two stores change is what a stall means when it arrives. A sentence that dies mid-delivery used to carry a single verdict, that the speaking needs more practice. Now it can carry an address, pointing at whichever store came up short this time, and the hour of study that follows has somewhere particular to go.
